The sound sh as in shoe is spelled with a single character, ʃ.

  • it makes more phoetic sense
  • it is more compact However, the sound ch as in chase is spelled Tʃ rather than with a single character. The sound nk is also spelled with two characters, ŊK.
  • this also makes phonetic sense, since ch and nk are really made up each of two sounds, t+sh and ng+k.
  • while this is not more compact, it is not less compact either.

Sometimes a less compact orthography might make more phonetic sense — as opposed to an orthography which is more compact or as compact, see above. For example, the letter x could be broken down into its composing sounds with two letters, such as k+s for axis or gz for exact. However, this is less compact than standard English spelling. In cases such as this, the English orthography is kept.

In other words, consonant orthography aims at aligning itself to english, accept in cases where an alternative both makes more phonetic sense and is more compact.

The aw sound, as in bark is often categorized as an a-vowel variant. However, since it is close to an O sound, and since the a already has a variant — the a-sound in aha! — it is categorized as an o-variant.

Not all vowel sounds are included. For example, the diphthong ey as in rain is not included. This diphthong is so similar in pronunciation to the sound long a (as well as short e + y) that it is spelled as a long a.
