This is my Handwriting Game

From the very beginning, the manuscript aspect of this script has been by far what interests me the most. It is always shifting and changing as I try to create an enjoyable and interesting writing experience for myself.

Alphabet

This script tries to be a bit more phonetically coherent that typical English spelling.

Now, that I don't take into account in creating the alphabet — this isn't the IPA. I'm trying to find a ballance between something that makes phonetic sense and something that isn't overly complex.

Consonants

Below are the 26 consonant sounds that I keep in for English. The idea is that the chart can be filled out and extended for different languages or different dialects of english.

There are three families of consonants based on how they are pronounced:

  • Front: pronounced with the lips
  • Middle: pronounced with teeth and soft palate
  • Back: pronounced with hard palate and throat

The chart uses five symbols for sounds which don't have their own letters in the English alphabet:

  • Θ: thanks
  • Ð: that
  • ʃ: shirt
  • Ʒ: vision
  • Ŋ: strong

The four compound sounds are pretty straightforward:

  • Tʃ: chair
  • DƷ: juice
  • KS: tax
  • GZ: exam

In each family, there are a few parent sounds (rows). Each parent sound can have multiple sounds in its family (columns):

  • Columns 1-2: contains the parent letter itself and the vocalized version of the parent letter, if there is one.
  • Columns 3-4: contains letters which are close in pronunciation to the parent letter and therefore placed within its family. They also are categorized as silent (col 3) and vocal (col 4).
  • Columns 5-6: contains compound sounds which begin with the parent letter or its vocal variant. They also are categorized as silent (col 5), and vocal (col 6).

In the script, the above details about a sound's pronunciation are inherent in how its letter is drawn. For example, letters with the same parent all have the same base character. This base character is modified with a vocal mark for vocal sounds, a compound mark for compound sounds, and so on.

Since the T family is the only family of sounds that covers all the different sound types, we'll use it as an example below:

First pair:

  • The T sound corresponds to a parent letter with the character t. This character has a stem on the right because it is pronounced at the very front of the mouth, like the letter T in English.
  • A vocalized T sound becomes a D sound. The D sound is represented with the T letter with a vocal mark, d.

Second pair:

  • The Θ sound as in "thanks" is somewhat similar to a T sound, so I put it in the T family. It is represented with a T letter with a "relative" mark, u.
  • A vocalized Θ sound becomes a Ð sound, as in "this." It is represented with a T letter with both a related mark and a vocal mark, a.

Third pair:

  • The Tʃ sound as in "chair" is in reality two sounds. The first of the two sounds is a T sound, so it belongs in the T family. It is represented by a T letter with a compound mark, e.
  • A vocalized Tʃ sound becomes a DƷ sound, as in "juice." It is represented with a T letter with a compound mark and a vocal mark, i.

Vowels

For years I've just kept english spelling when it comes to vowels. Now, I wonder if it wouldn't be interesting to go in a more phonetic direction here too.

All rules about vowels are extremely tentative at this point.

Below are the vowel sounds that I keep in mind. Again, I have tried to categorize them according to a parent.

The letter Y is not a parent since the vowel sounds it makes fall under the I family. The double letters OO are a parent because otherwise there are just too many sounds in the O family!

parent short long r-mod diphthong other
A tan cake bark aha
E pen feet
I nib ride oil
O hop rope fork owl raw
U mud muse loud
OO room took

19 vowels is a bit much to keep track of...

First, I think some sounds don't have to have their own distinct symbol.

  • R-mod vowels probably don't need their own symbols since the presence of an r serves to point them out.
  • The i-diphthong and the o-diphthong probably don't need their own symbols either. I like the idea of having a symbol for the u diphthong.

For the remaining sounds... I wonder if it's workable to use a single letter for each family in most writing. There could be a way to indicate which exact vowel type a vowel is, but it will probably hard to use this in a practical way during normal writing, so ideally it shouldn't be necessary most of the time.
