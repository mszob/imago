alphabet

There are three families of consonants based on where they are pronounced in the mouth. Each family is represented with a general letter design.

  • Front: pronounced with the lips
  • Middle: pronounced with teeth and soft palate
  • Back: pronounced with hard palate and throat

In each family, there are a few parent sounds. Each parent sound represents a distinct glyph.

Finally, each parent letter can have several derived letters

  • silent: this is the parent letter itself
  • vocal: this is a vocalized version of the parent letter
  • related: these are letters which are close enough in pronunciation to the parent letter that they have been grouped with it.
  • compound: this is technically two separate sounds, the first of which is the parent letter or its vocal variant

Example

  • The T sound is a parent letter with its own glyph, t. It is pronounced like the T in english, with the tongue placed on the hard palate.
    • A vocalized T sound becomes a D sound. The D sound is represented with the T letter with a vocal mark, d.
  • The TH sound as in "thissle" (Θ) is very similar to a T sound. It is represented with a T letter with a related mark, u.
    • A vocalized Θ sound becomes a Ð sound as in "this." It is represented with a T letter with a related mark and a vocal mark, i.
  • The Tʃ sound as in "chair" is in reality two sound, the first of which is a T sound. It is represented by a T letter with a compound mark
    • A vocalized Tʃ sound becomes a DƷ sound, as in "juice." It is represented with a T letter with a compound mark and a vocal mark

The sound sh as in shoe is spelled with a single character, ʃ.

  • it makes more phoetic sense
  • it is more compact However, the sound ch as in chase is spelled Tʃ rather than with a single character. The sound nk is also spelled with two characters, ŊK.
  • this also makes phonetic sense, since ch and nk are really made up each of two sounds, t+sh and ng+k.
  • while this is not more compact, it is not less compact either.

Sometimes a less compact orthography might make more phonetic sense — as opposed to an orthography which is more compact or as compact, see above. For example, the letter x could be broken down into its composing sounds with two letters, such as k+s for axis or gz for exact. However, this is less compact than standard English spelling. In cases such as this, the English orthography is kept.

In other words, consonant orthography aims at aligning itself to english, accept in cases where an alternative both makes more phonetic sense and is more compact.

The aw sound, as in bark is often categorized as an a-vowel variant. However, since it is close to an O sound, and since the a already has a variant — the a-sound in aha! — it is categorized as an o-variant.

Not all vowel sounds are included. For example, the diphthong ey as in rain is not included. This diphthong is so similar in pronunciation to the sound long a (as well as short e + y) that it is spelled as a long a.
